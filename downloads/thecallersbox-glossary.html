People

Contra corners (people)

The first contra corners are the people on the right diagonal. (At the start of the contra corners figure, when everyone's facing across.) The second contra corners are the people on the left diagonal. This is similar to first corners and second corners in English Country Dance, where it's the people on the right/left diagonal within the minor set.

Corners (square)

Often, "corner" is a convention for naming people in a square, normally used as a relative position rather than a specific person. ("Places, not faces".) In this database, however, it's used as a specific person. Your partner is your partner. All corners are of the opposite role. The non-partner next to you is your corner C1. (This is the standard corner position.) The person across from you ("opposite") is your corner C2. The remaining person is your corner C3.

This notation is also used in a four-face-four. If at the start of the dance, you took hands in a circle of eight, you'd be next to C1. Other corners are determined by imagining the circle to be a square. (Rotated 45 degrees, but yes, a square.)

A standard grand right and left is notated (PR;C3L;C2R;C1L)

A slight variation of this notation is used to describe progressions in circle mixers. C1 progression means your future partner is standing next to you, when all are facing in as couples, with men on their current partner's left. C-1 progression means your future partner is on the far side of your current partner. (In circle formations where you're not all in a big circle facing the center, men's C1 corner is the next person counterclockwise around the entire set. Women's C1 corner is the next person clockwise around the entire set.)

First/second corners

A convention from proper English Country Dance. First corners are man one and woman two. Second corners are woman one and man two. In this database, by default these refer to people, rather than positions. First/second corner notation is mostly used to describe unusual hey passes.

In a few dances, there is a reference to "first corner positions", especially if it's unclear who will be there due to dancers' choices. In that case, "first corner positions" means whoever is in the position of man one and woman two (from original proper hands-four). It can also be thought of as those on the right diagonal within a duple minor set, when everyone is facing across.

Men

The traditional label of the role of one of the people in a couple. Nowadays more commonly called "gents" or "larks," as well as a few more obscure names.

The men's role finishes on the left side after a swing, unless otherwise noted in the dance instructions.

Neighbors

People who change after each repetition of the dance. (Exception: Circle mixers, where they're labelled "partners", because that's the term everyone uses.) This can refer to either a couple ("Circle left with neighbors") or a single person. If a single person ("Neighbor swing"), the default assumption is that it's with the opposite role person. Same-role neighbors are typically labelled.

If you have multiple neighbors, they're numbered. Your current neighbor is N1. Your next neighbor in the direction of progression is N2. This is followed by N3, N4, and so forth. Your previous neighbor is N0. The neighbor before that is N-1, and so forth.

If you're dealing with multiple neighbors and your shadow is in your hands-four, things get a little hairy. The numbering for neighbor refers to your opposite-role neighbor. Your same-role neighbor has a different neighbor number. This is typically omitted, especially if notating it is more confusing than not notating it.

You'll see both "neighbor N2" and "N2 neighbor" used. The two varieties are to keep the "N2" as far away as possible from the other search terms. So there's "N2 neighbor allemande left" or "Right and left through with neighbor N2."

Ones/Twos

Within a minor set, the couple that starts closest to the music is the ones. (The exception is reverse progression dances.) The second couple is the twos. In a triple minor, the third couple is the threes.

In this database, ones are defined as the couple progressing down the set. Twos progress up the set. Be careful of the following:

  • Becket dances with ones/twos. Check the progression direction (cw vs ccw) to see who is progressing down the set.
  • Four facing four. Both couples facing down are ones. Both couples facing up are twos.
  • Reverse progression improper. In this case, I'm defining the top couples in a hands-four as the twos, since they're progressing up the set.
  • Sicilian circles. Ones progress counterclockwise, twos progress clockwise.

Opposite

At the beginning of a four-face-four (or a three-face-three), this is the neighbor you're facing. (The neighbor on your diagonal in a four-face-four is instead called your C2 corner.

Partner

Someone in your original minor set that stays with you as you progress. This is probably the person you asked to dance with. Some formations (like three-frace-threes) have multiple partners.

The exception to this rule is circle mixers, where your "partner" is the temporary person you're dancing with.

Partners (mixers)

Your current partner is P1. The next partner in your direction of progression is P2, then P3, and so forth. Your previous partner is P0, the one before that P-1, and so forth.

Phantoms

Ghost (non-existant) dancers needed to complete a move. For instance, if only two dancers in a singlet were turning contra corners, they would need to pretend other phantom dancers existed to make the figure work.

Primarily used in very small set dances.

Same-role

i.e. Same gender role. Men with men, and women with women. If just men or just women are involved (as in "Men allemande left"), then same-role will not be used.

Shadows

Someone who is always in your hands-four at that point of the dance, who is not your partner. They take the role of your partner within your hands-four.

If there are multiple shadows, they'll be numbered. Shadow S1 is the first shadow you encounter one hands-four away from your partner. S2 is one hands-four beyond that, and so forth. The S-1 shadow is the first shadow in the other direction -- if the S1 shadow is in the hands four above you, then the S-1 shadow is in the hands four below you.

Currently S1 versus S-1 does not indicate the direction the shadow is found.

There is no shadow S0. That'd be your partner.

Trail buddy

In a four facing four, it's the opposite gender role person in the couple traveling with you. (A shadow in a four-face-four is someone from another minor set of eight.)

(In other sources, "trail buddy" is a synonym for shadow. I'm redefining the term for this database.)

Women

The traditional label of the role of one of the people in a couple. Nowadays more commonly called "ladies" or "robins," as well as a few more obscure names.

The women's role finishes on the right side after a swing, unless otherwise noted in the dance instructions.

Formations: Orientations

Across/along

Along: Facing the direction of progression. For instance, when facing your neighbor at the start of an improper dance. Or the starting direction of "in lines of four, go forward and back" in a four-face-four.

Across: Facing perpendicular to the direction of progression. During "In long lines, go forward and back", you're facing across the set. Most ladies chains are done across the set.

Arky

Gender-swapped. An arky right-and-left through would start with the man on the right, woman on the left, and all performing the other dancer's role.

History

Comes from 1950's square dancing, where there was a caller from Arkansas whose schtick was often doing normal calls from unfamiliar gender-role positions.

Originally, "Arky" referred to any situation not in standard gender-role positions, so a same-role right and left through would be considered arky. However, in this database, "Arky" refers only to positions where the man's role is on the right, woman's role is on the left.

Center

The region of the set between the two lines, or the people in the center of the set. For instance, the center two people in a line of four. Or the place where ones swing during "Chorus Jig".

Clockwise

The same rotational direction as a circle left or allemande right. Clockwise becket formation dances progress to the left.

Counterclockwise

The same rotational direction as a circle right or allemande left. Counterclockwise becket formation dances progress to the right.

Diagonal

In contras, right/left diagonal first assumes you're first facing across the set. Then you look on the appropriate diagonal.

In other formations it gets a little more context-dependent. In four-face-fours, or three-face threes, it depends on the previous figure(s), but most likely it first assumes you're facing along the set.

Down/up

"Up" is towards the top of the hall, where the band is traditionally placed. "Down" is away from the top of the hall.

Form

A verb meaning to create a specific formation (line of four, long waves, ring, etc.) in the most obvious way possible.

Middle

Neither the top nor bottom couple of the set. For instance, in a quadruplet, the twos and threes are middle couples.

Major set

The entire contra line.

More specifically, the collection of all minor sets that a dancer could progress between during the normal course of a dance. (Exception: For grid contras, the major set is just one contra line.)

For contras, this is sometimes also called "entire set".

Minor set

For a typical contra, a hands-four of four dancers.

More generally, a group of dancers that interact with each other during part of a dance before progressing onto a new similar group of dancers. Not all formation have minor sets -- for instance triplets don't have them.

Some examples:

  • Duple minor contra: Group of four dancers.
  • Triple minor contra: Group of six dancers.
  • Four facing four: Group of eight dancers.
  • Circle mixer: Pair of two dancers.
  • Zia: Either a hand-four or a square of eight, depending on the context.

Sicilian circle

A contra line bent into a circle, so that its two ends are connected. Ones progress counterclockwise, twos progress clockwise. Since couples are never out, there are no end effects.

Other formations can be made into a Sicilian circle. The most common are four facing fours and three facing threes

Formations: Arrangements of dancers

Column

A line of four across the set, but not holding hands. Typically everyone is facing across.

Very similar to starting in a wave of four.

Diamond

A ring of four rotated 45 degrees. See "Fiddleheads" or the modern interpretation of "Petronella" for examples. This has nothing to do with the MWSD definition of diamond formation.

This can sometimes be a starting formation.

Interlocking waves

Two long waves (typically in the center of the set) that are on top of each other, and intersect as every handhold. An example would be from "Woven Waves" where all the men are in the center, forming a long wave, and all the women are in the center, forming their own long wave. The waves cross at every pair of handholds.

Intersecting waves

Two waves that cross at only one point. The waves are typically perpendicular to each other, and share a common center. An example would be the waves in "Quick Spin".

This can be extended to multiple waves, like a long wave in a contra set, with intersecting waves of two, as in "Our House". Each wave intersects another wave in a maximum of one location.

Line of four

A line of four people, all facing the same way. (Waves of four are not classified as lines of four in this database.) Typically used in "in a line of four, go down the hall," and followed by parentheses that list the order of people in the line (M1-W2-M2-W1). This ordering is from the perspective of the caller at the top of the hall. This matters for the "up the hall in a line of four", where these are two different possible perspectives.

Long wave

Either one long wave in the center, or (default) two long waves on each side. Parentheses often show who has which hands with whom (NR), and who is facing in to the center of the set.

Ring

A non-moving circle of four, aligned the same way as the original hands-four. If you rotate it 45 degrees, you get a diamond.

Two-faced line

An unusual formation of four people in a generalized line of four. It's one pair facing the same way, and another pair facing the other way. The most common usage is in a star promenade.

The name is taken from MWSD, where it's featured a lot more.

Wave of four

This is almost always followed by parentheses describing the wave, like (NR,WL).

The first element describes the pairing at the ends of the wave of four -- what their relationship is and which hand they connect with. The second element describes the pairing in the center -- who and with which hand.

None of this defines the balance direction. The balance direction is usually omitted, though there might be a second parenthetical comment using a semi-colon, like (L;R) or (R;back).

Formations: Duple minor

Becket

Starting the dance on the side of the set with your partner.

An "indecent Becket" starts with you next to your partner, except when facing across, the man is on the right, the woman is on the left.

A "proper Becket" starts with you next to your partner, except all the men are above the women, or vice versa. See the dance notes for who's on first.

Cross formation

A very unusual hands-four formation where people are diagonal from their partner. One example would be to take proper formation, then have the women trade. See the notes in the relevant dances for details.

Duple minor

A fancy way of describing a contra dance.

"Duple" refers to the number of couples in a minor set -- two couples.

The minor set is the group you do one round of the dance with (ignoring leaving the minor set with diagonal chains and such). So, it's the hands-four dancers take before starting the dance.

The major set is the entire long line of a contra dance, composed of many minor sets/hands fours.

Improper

The most common duple minor contra formation. From proper formation, ones trade places with their partner.

"Progressed improper" takes improper formation, has everyone trade places with their neighbor (for instance, after a neighbor swing), and stay within the same hands-four.

"All improper" means both the ones and twos are crossed over.

In a big circle (circle mixer), it means that when facing into the center, the man's role is on the left and the woman's role on the right.

Indecent

The mirror image of improper. From a proper formation, twos trade places with their partner.

"Progressed indecent" takes indecent formation, has everyone trade places with their neighbor, and stay within the same hands-four. (It's equivalent to reverse progression improper.)

In a big circle (circle mixer), it means the woman's role is on the left, the man's role on the right.

Proper

The original contra dance formation, with all the men on one side and all the women on the other.

If all cross over to begin, you get the "All improper" formation.

Progressed improper

From improper formation, trade places with your neighbor, but stay with the same hands-four. (This is equivalent to reverse progression indecent.)

There is also "progressed indecent," which is equivalent to reverse progression improper. There's probably even "progressed proper" lurking out there somewhere.

Reverse progression improper

Improper, but the couple closer to the music progresses up the set, and the couple further from the music progresses down the set.

Ones are still the couples progressing down the set, so any notation that refers to ones or twos assumes the ones start below the twos.

This formation is the same as progressed indecent.

Formations: Other Formations

Circle mixer

There's a couple of different starting arrangements for circle mixers.

The standard one is where all are facing in to the center of the circle. Within partnerships, men are on the left and women are on the right.

Another is two concentric circles, all facing their partner. The men are in the inner circle, facing out, while the women are in the outer circle facing in. (Interestingly enough, there's only one dance that has those roles swapped.)

A C1 progression means men single progress clockwise, women single progress counterclockwise. (In the direction of your corner.)

A C-2 progression means women double progress clockwise, men double progress counterclockwise. (In the opposite direction of your corner.)

Circle of threesomes

Not a three facing three in a sicilian circle.

This is actually a circle mixer, where instead of couples, you have threesomes.

At some point during the dance, one or more people progress on to another threesome. In some dances, people can change roles/positions within the threesome.

Double quadrille

Doublet

A dance for only two couples.

If there is a progression, with each round of the dance, the previous round's neighbor becomes this round's partner, and verse vica. The "top of the hall" direction also rotates 90 degrees each time.

While there are a few dances that were deliberately written as doublets, many improper contras can also be done as a doublet with this kind of progression.

Other doublets are modified contras deliberately written without progression, so they can be done with very small groups.

Five couple square

A square with a fifth couple in the center. Couples are numbered normally, except the center couple is couple number five. (Couple one is the top couple, and then couples are numbered counterclockwise around the square.) At some point during the dance sequence, everyone progresses to a new position.

Four facing four

A contra where instead of couples facing couples, there are lines of four facing other lines of four. Minor sets are in groups of eight.

The other couple in your line of four is your trail buddy, and travel with you as you progress. In many four-face-fours, you swap sides with this trail buddy every time through the dance sequence.

The neighbor you begin facing is your opposite neighbor. See also corner notation for describing people. Ones still progress down. Twos still progress up.

There's several variations of the four facing four:

  • Tempest formation, where the ones' line of four is between the twos, and the twos are split up, facing across at the sides.
  • Sicilian four-face-fours, which is the combination of a four facing four and a sicilian circle.
  • Hands-four beckets, where after setting up the four-face-four formation, groups of four circle 1/4
  • Hands-eight beckets, where after setting up the four-face-four formation, all eight circle 1/4.
  • The "Double Chocolate" formation, where instead of a line of four, you have two couples, one behind each other, but both facing and progressing in the same direction.
  • And the "Kim's Game" formation, where two of the couples progress to other minor sets, but two stay put, or only vibrate back and forth between two minor sets.

"Four facing four" is also sometimes called "mescolanza" or "double contra."

Grid contra

A contra where you progress both up/down and across the hall. The original contra lines should be aligned so the hands-fours are equally spaced. Minor sets consist of two couples.

Progression happens either within the major longways set, or between major longways sets. Or both.

Grid square

A square where you progress between squares.

Squares are arranged in a rectangular grid. At some point during the dance sequence, some or all couples progress to another square. Often there's an intermediate step where they dance with a hands-four with another couple that progressed out of an adjacent square.

A single progression in a grid square is going from the location of one square to the location of an adjacent square, either vertically or horizontally.

Hexagon

A square for six couples.

There are two kinds of hexagons. One has two couples in each head position, for a total of four head couples. There are also a total of two side couples.

The other kind is where there are three head couples and three side couples. The roles alternate around the hexagon. Many figures get done differently.

Horseshoe

The formation for "Levi Jackson Rag."

A longways set where the top couple is improper, and other couples are becket. Typically there are hands-four figures, whole-set figures, and some sort of progression.

Longways set

In this database, "longways set" as an informal formation name means a straight line of couples without minor sets. So, "Virginia Reel" and "Galopede" are examples of longways sets, where couples dance as a whole set. A contra is not considered a "longways set" because the line is divided up into minor sets of hands-fours.

Some longways sets have a specified number of couples, others are for as many as will.

Other

This glossary is not an exhaustive list of formations. There's others in the database -- read the Dance Notes or the original source material for more information on the more exotic formations.

Quadruplet

A four-couple longways set. This can also be thought of as an extension of a triplet.

Couples are numbered 1, 2, 3, and 4. A proper quadruplet means all the men are on one side, and all the women are on the other. If some couples are crossed over (improper), it's listed which couples are crossed over.

The progression shows the location of each couple at the end of one time through the dance. The left-most digit represents the top of the hall.

Scatter mixer

Minor sets are scattered around the room, without any overall organization or grouping into major sets. At some point in the dance, the sets dissolve, and people progress randomly to create new minor sets.

The progression (single/double) refers to the number of partner changes during once through the dance.

Singlet

A dance for only one couple that still primarily uses contra figures.

Six facing six

A four facing four variant where the minor set is a line of three couples facing another line of three couples.

Solo

A dance for only one person, yet still resembling a contra dance because one is doing contra figures with phantoms.

Written for individual remote (home) dancing using group meeting software (like Zoom) during the quarantine of the COVID-19 pandemic that began in 2020.

Square

Not included in this database. Sorry.

Though some unusual square variants are included, like double quadrilles, five couple squares, and hexagons.

Tempest

A sub-class of four facing fours. From a standard four-face-four formation, the twos separate away from each other, and face across at each other. The line of four of the ones steps forward until they're between the twos.

Twos still progress up, ones progress down. The common progression is to have the ones go down the hall, then return, but only as far as new neighbors. Meanwhile, twos shift up the hall to prevent set drift.

Three facing three

A contra where the partnership is three people, rather than two.

Threesomes face threesomes, all facing in the direction of their progression. You begin facing your opposite neighbor.

The order of the people in your threesome (who is the left-end, center, or right-end person) can stay constant through repetitions of the dance, or can vary. Check the dance instructions for details.

Tri-contra

A becket mixer variant of a three facing three, where the center people of the threesomes never progress.

Most of these were written for a man in the center of a partnership of three, with a woman on either side of him. The dance is done facing another threesome. After once through the dance, the women progress, typically to the next women's position around the major set. So the left-hand woman would become the right-hand woman in the same threesome, and the right-hand woman would become the left-hand woman in the adjacent threesome on the same side.

Further Reading

Triple minor

A contra with a hands-six minor set rather than a hands-four minor set. Couples within the minor set are labelled ones, twos, and threes.

Ones progress down; twos and threes progress up. The number of progressions refers to the ones -- how many couples they pass. In a single progression dance, ones will progress down past the twos, and take new hands six. The twos in the new hands-six were threes in their previous hands-six.

If improper, it's listed which couples are crossed over.

When dancing a triple minor, when a couple reaches the top of the set, they have to wait out twice until there's a full hands-six for them to dance with. At the bottom of the set, if there's only a hands-four, the ones need to progress to the bottom, or else the bottom couple will never get back in the dance.

Triplet

A three-couple longways set, introduced to contra dance by Ted Sannella.

Couples are numbered 1, 2, and 3. A proper triplet means all the men are on one side, and all the women are on the other. If some couples are crossed over (improper), it's listed which couples are crossed over.

Progression is usually "312" or "231." The progression shows the location of each couple at the end of one time through the dance. The left-most digit represents the top of the hall.

Further reading

Zia

A square/contra hybrid. Two or more (typically improper) contra lines intersect at right angles. Their intersections form squares.

Couples move down a contra line, then reach a square, progress to a different location in a square, and then enter another contra line radiating from that square.

Most zias are double progression. After the first progression, the square(s) disappear, as all the dancers in the square face away to form hands-fours with people in the contra lines. After the second progression, the square(s) re-form.

Ideally the same figures are done in the contra hands-fours and the squares, but just in a different context, so a ladies chain turns into a ladies grand chain. Or, circle left four steps is circle left 1/2 in a hands-four, but circle left 1/4 in the squares.

In an individual line of text, the contra move appears first, followed by the punctuation || followed by the square move.

If there is only one move, then either the move is the same (petronella turn), or because dancers are only singly progressed, and there are no squares.

Allemande

Description

A hand turn, done in a particular style. (Arm-wrestling grip).

Notation

The exact distance (within a quarter rotation) is fuzzy, since it depends on when you start and end the allemande -- for instance when an allemande on the side switches to a half hey across. It can also depend upon adjustments of inactive dancers. However, hopefully the number of beats for the allemande and the figures before and after can help.

On the sides of the contra set, I've tried to be precise about how far it is done. I usually count the turning from when you are first close to the other person. So, the MWSD move "touch a quarter" (with person you're facing, walk forward till you're right shoulder to right shoulder, then right-hand turn a quarter) I annotate as "allemande right 1/2".

In the center of the set, I've used multiples of 1/2. If the fractional part is 1/2, then someone crosses the set. If there's no fraction, people return to their original side. If the fraction is 3/4, someone either started or finished in the center of the set. The reason for this is that the non-allemanding people often shift or adjust while the others are allemanding in the center, defeating any chance to measure allemandes to the exact eighths.

Sample text

  • Men allemande left 1/2
  • N2 same-role neighbor allemande left 1
  • Neighbor allemande left (M1+W2 1 & 1/4, W1+M2 3/4)
  • Neighbor allemande right 1 [M1 with N2, W1 with N1]
  • Neighbor mirror allemande 1 (ones split twos)

Description

A composite figure. Allemande left someone, face away from them, pass/pull by the next person by the right, and the next-next person swing.

History

Western square dance callers in the 1950's developed circular choreography around the outside of the square like grand right and left to keep everyone moving. A number of allemande variants popped up. To read far more than you ever wanted to know about this, check out the allemande alphabet article on the Square Dance History page.

A few of these have survived to occasionally be used in traditional squares. The opening moves of allemande X even shows up in some contras and circle mixers.

  • Allemande X:
    (6) N0 neighbor allemande left 1
    (2) Pass through along (N1R)

Description

This is a chase figure. One couple trades places with their partner by going around another standing couple. The lead person goes around the other couple completely, while their partner follows, only going around one person, each finishing in their partner's place.

Example

This comes from southern square dancing, then was introduced to contra dancing by Ted Sannella in "New Friendship Reel".

Celtic knot

Description

A hey on two perpendicular axes, or two spin the tops without hands.

It starts in wave of four positions. People at the ends allemande right 1/2 or right shoulder round 1/2. Then the centers left shoulder round3/4 while the others orbit clockwise 1/4. All meet the same person, in wave of four positions -- though this wave is perpendicular to the original wave. Now right shoulder round 1/2 the same person, and then new centers left shoulder round 3/4 while new ends orbit clockwise 1/4.

There's also a mirror left-handed version of this.

History

First appears in "The Tropical Gentleman" by Kathy Anderson.

  • Celtic knot:
    (2) Partner pass right
    (4) Men left shoulder round 3/4 || Women orbit clockwise 1/4
    (2) Partner pass right
    (4) Women left shoulder round 3/4 || Men orbit clockwise 1/4

Circulate

Description

Individually advance to the next person's position in your defined group. Practically, this is done in long waves. In your group of four, the people facing in walk straight across the set, and stay facing out to form new long waves. It should define who crosses, who loops, and in which direction.

Contra corners

Description

A series of four allemandes done by the named couple. When facing across, you have someone straight across from you. (Typically this is your partner. If it's someone else, like neighbor, the contra corners will be defined as "with" that person.) Your "first contra corner" is the person on your right diagonal from your starting position. Your "second contra corner" is the person on your left diagonal from your starting position.

Contra corners is then a series of allemandes led by the active people:

  • Person across allemande right 5/8
  • First contra corner allemande left 1
  • Original person across allemande right 3/4
  • Second contra corner allemande left 1

Notation

Contra corners ends in an unusual configuration. Typically, it's followed by a swing in the center, or a "return to place", which means return to sidelines you started from. (Original active people pass left shoulders as they cross the set.) But if not, be careful.

History

This was originally a triple minor figure, where it's a lot easier to comprehend. Also, the hands used vary over the centuries.

Sample text

  • Ones turn contra corners; return to place

Courtesy turn

Description

The second half of right and left through, promenade across, and ladies chain. It's also a way to turn as a couple.

A couple faces in the same direction and rotates counterclockwise around a central pivot point until facing the direction needed for the next move. (Typically halfway. If all the way around, this is sometimes called a "power turn".) The person on the left (typically the man) backs up, the person on the right (typically the woman) walks forward. The usual handhold is woman's right hand on her right hip, man's right hand behind her back, connected to that right hand. Left hands are connected in front.

With the gents chain, there is a clockwise mirror image of this figure (man has left hand on his left hip.)

Defaults

This figure is assumed and not included for all chains/right and left throughs/promenades across, and is only listed when it is a separate figure. So searching for "courtesy turn" won't pick up most of the dances with that figure.

Unless otherwise specified, all courtesy turns are counterclockwise.

Sample text

  • Partner courtesy turn

Cozy line

Description

An intermediate stage between a line of four and a cloverleaf. Start from a line of four facing down and keep hands throughout. Centers form an arch, then they turn away from each other, the centers going under their own hands backwards, then bring those connected hands in front of them. Throughout this, the ends maintain their existing hand-hold with the centers. With their free hand the ends reach behind the centers and take that free hand with each other.

You now have a cozy line, which goes up the hall. To form the cloverleaf, the original ends raise their joined hands to make an arch, the centers back up underneath the arch, and the ends bring the arch comes down in front of the centers.

History

First introduced into contra with "Symmetrical Force" by Fred Feild.

Sample text

  • Form cozy line: Holding hands ones turn away from each other, bring hands over heads; twos join hands behind

Cross

Description

Someone crosses the set on a straight path. Unlike trade or pass through, you are not exchanging places with another person coming straight at you.

This is typically done by walking straight across the set. However, a diagonal cross is possible, as in "Lamplighter's Hornpipe" or the first part of a half figure eight. In that case, people diagonally cross, going to the other side while the others adjust (if needed) by moving down or up one place.

Notation

Trade is two people exchanging places on a diagonal. Pass is two people exchanging places across or along the set, coming close to each other. In cross, you're not closely passing by someone.

These are only distinctions relevant for this database, to better search for specific patterns. Use whatever description you like when teaching the dance.

Sample text

  • Women cross by right
  • Ones cross diagonally down below twos, twos move up

See also "Couples do-si-do", "seesaw", and "mad robin".

Defaults

Once around, unless otherwise specified.

In a mirror do-si-do, half the people do-si-do, and the other half seesaw.

Sample text

  • Neighbor mirror do-si-do (ones split twos)
  • Neighbor do-si-do 1 & 1/4; form wave of four
  • Neighbor do-si-do; form long wave
  • Partner do-si-do (in center)

Do-si-do, couples

Description

Connect up with someone facing the same direction and hold nearest hand (or possibly promenade position). As a unit do-si-do the other couple you're facing.

Notation

"As couples, neighbor do-si-do" means you're likely connected with your partner, and the two of you do-si-do your neighbor couple.

Sample text

  • As couples, neighbor do-si-do

Dolphin hey

Description

A hey for three, where one of the three actors is a couple acting in tandem, with one closely following the other person. This couple swaps the lead whenever they loop around at the end of a hey, usually with the current lead person looping extra wide, and the trailing person cutting in front of them. Typically done across the set. Typically starts with the pair in the center of the hey axis.

Notation

Parentheses first list the pair of people, then whom they pass to begin the hey, and by what shoulder.

History

Comes from Scottish Country Dancing. From there, Mary Devlin used it in the ECD dance, "Halsway Manners", where it spread to ECD. A few people have experimented with it in contra.

Defaults

Finishes where it starts. Each pass is considered 1/6 of a hey. Done across the set.

Sample text

  • Dolphin hey 1 & 1/6 (ones pass W2L to begin)

Double figure eight

Description

Both the ones and twos simultaneously start a figure eight, though they begin it at different parts. One couples begins by crossing down/up through the set, while the other casts up/down on the sides.

History

Comes from modern interpretations in ECD.

Example

"Double figure eight (ones cast down, twos cross up):" The ones cast down one place, diagonally cross up through the center (W1 before M1), cast down the other side one place, and diagonally cross up through the center (W1 before M1) to original place. Meanwhile the twos cross up, cast down, cross up, and cast down to original place.

Notation

Parentheses indicate the starting move.

The full figure has four pieces. It can be fractioned, especially the double figure eight 1/2.

Sample text

  • Double figure eight (ones cross down, twos cast up)
  • Double figure eight 1/2 (ones cross up, twos cast down)

Double gyp

Description

A fast-paced variant of corner trades.

People on the diagonals rapidly trade places, crossing as soon as the other diagonal has crossed. This can also be thought of as interlocking shoulder rounds. In the original version, one diagonal pair passes by one shoulder, the other diagonal pair passes by the other shoulder.

Notation

The number afterwards gives the number of diagonal trades. Individual passes are listed afterwards in the indented text block.

History

Introduced by Larry Jennings in Zesty Contras as an attempt to add new figures to contra dance. He probably got it from "Long Live London" by Pat Shaw. Pat Shaw may have gotten it from Morris dancing.

Sample text

  • Corner trade 4 (Double gyp):
    (2) Women trade by left
    (2) Men trade by right
    (2) Women trade by left
    (2) Men trade by right

Double slice

Description

A variant of slice that progresses two couples. Face a couple on the diagonal, go forward towards them in four steps, as couples pivot so you're facing the other diagonal, and fall back from them. Then face across so you're facing yet another couple, double progressed.

History

Also termed "Yearn" by George Walker.

Sample text

  • Double slice left:
    (4) On left diagonal, go forward to N1
    (4) On other diagonal, fall back to N2

Down outside

Description

The noted people (typically the ones) separate to the nearest outside of the major set, and individually walk down the outside of the major set.

Notation

If the choreography is down the outside, and next come up the outside to place, no distance details are given. If it's go down the outside a certain distance (like past two couples), the people you go past are noted in parentheses.

The difference between "down outside" and "cast down" is fairly arbitrary, though "down outside" is more likely to be in the direction you're already facing, while "cast down" is more likely to be opposite the direction you're facing. The database is sloppy as to the distinction between these two. Sorry.

History

Most commonly seen in "Chorus Jig". This was a common traditional figure in the 1800's.

Sample text

  • Ones go down outside
  • Ones lead down || Twos go up outside

Draw poussette

Description

A poussette variant. The path is more of a circle (or broken circle), but only holding on to one person.

Take two hands with the designated person and orbit the other couple. However, one person always walks backwards, the other always walks forward. This means that your hold with the other person rotates with respect to the room.

History

A modern variant of the poussette developed by 20th century ECD.

Sample text

  • Neighbor draw poussette clockwise 1/2

Eggbeater

Description

Simultaneous allemandes in the center. As an example, women allemande left. Between the pairs of women, men from a different minor set allemande right. The timing of the allemandes is offset so that only one pair is crowding the center at the same time.

History

The first known use is several dances by Cary Ravitz in 1995 ("Pinwheels", "The Enchanted Forrest", "Rat Race"). Named by Bill Olson, who also wrote several dances with this.

Sample text

  • Eggbeater: N1 men allemande left 1 & 1/2 || N2 women allemande right 1 & 1/2

This wording is often used in ECD. Not so much in contra, but I need it to annotate certain dances.

  • Men walk forward; form long wave in center || Women fall back

Facing star

Description

A strange star variant where half the people are walking backwards. In the group of four, two people on one diagonal take right hands. The other two people take left hands. Often you then join your remaining hand with the person you're facing. The whole structure rotates, with two people walking backwards, the other two forwards.

History

Developed by Cary Ravitz in the dance "Gypsy Star". Originally called a "gypsy star."

Notation

Parentheses show who's on the diagonal, what hand they're holding in the center, and who they're facing.

Sample text

  • Facing star clockwise 3/4 [with N2] (ML, WR, free hand to partner)

Flutterwheel

Description

When facing across, the right-hand people (typically the women) right-hand turn 1/2 to the other side. When they reach the opposite person, they form a weak star-promenade-type position with them. (Center person's left hand with end person's right hand.) (Centers keep right hands with each other.) They then continue to the other side, centers letting go of right hands and bending the line to face across.

The net result of this is the left-hand people have traded places, while the right-hand people are back where they started.

History

This is an MWSD square dance figure. It appears often in MWSD contras but has never successfully made the leap to traditional contras.

Notation

Across the set. While one of the pieces is listed as a star promenade, that's only a loose approximation of the hand-hold. There is no butterfly whirl.

Sample text

  • Neighbor flutterwheel:
    (4) Women allemande right 1/2
    (4) Neighbor star promenade 1/2 (WR) (hand-in-hand with neighbor)

Forward and back

Description

In some formation, walk forward, then fall back to place.

Notation

Usually written with the formation name, then the text ", go forward and back." Typically, it's four counts to go forward, and four counts backwards.

Sample text

  • In a square, go forward and back
  • As couples, go forward and back (along the set)
  • In lines of four, go forward and back
  • In long lines, go forward and back

Gate

Description

Two people face the same direction, hold nearest hands, and pivot around the central point. One person backs up while the other person walks forwards. This is similar to a hand cast.

History

An import from ECD.

Sample text

  • Neighbor mirror gate 1 (ones forward)
  • Partner gate counterclockwise 1

Gay Gordons promenade

Description

For this promenade variant, the ideal handhold is hands at shoulder level, with man's right arm behind the woman's back connecting to the woman's right hand, and left hands in front. Promenade four steps forward, then individually pivot 180 degrees clockwise. Now the man is on the right, woman on the left, with hands still at shoulder level, left hands behind and right hands in front. Promenade like this four steps backwards. Then promenade four steps forwards in the same position, pivot, and promenade backwards to original place.

History

This is taken from the couples dance, "The Gay Gordons."

Sample text

  • Gay Gordons promenade:
    (4) P1 partner promenade counterclockwise; turn alone
    (4) P1 partner promenade counterclockwise (backwards)
    (4) P1 partner promenade clockwise; turn alone
    (4) P1 partner promenade clockwise (backwards)

Gents chain

Description

This is the mirror image of a ladies chain, where the left-hand people (men) trade places. Men pull by left (while women shift left to take their place), men veer a little more to their left, and on the other side do a clockwise courtesy turn. (Man is on the left, woman on the right. Man has left hand by his left hip, woman reaches behind with left hand to connect. Man walks forward, woman backs up.)

Sample text

  • Gents chain to partner

Georgia rang tang

Description

A series of arm turns and passes. Two people (for example, the women) stay on their side of the set. The other two people (for example, the men) cross the set passing by one shoulder, then turn the person they find by the same hand. Then they (the men) pass by the other shoulder and turn the person on the other side of the set by that same other hand. The pattern can repeat and is typically very fast.

Notation

Individual passes and turns are included in the description.

History

Comes from Appalachian squares.

Sample text

  • Georgia Rang Tang:
    (4) Neighbor allemande right 3/4
    (2) Men pass left
    (4) Partner allemande left 1
    (2) Men pass right
    (4) Neighbor allemande right 1
    (2) Men pass left
    (4) Partner allemande left 1
    (2) Men pass right

Give and take

Description

When facing another couple, take two steps forward. Then take two hands with the person across from you, and one person brings the other straight back to the sidelines. It is usually followed by a swing.

History

Invented by Larry Jennings as a swing/swing connector. The move is meant to only take four beats.

Notation

The named person ("women give-and-take") is the person who steps backwards in the second part of the figure, pulling the other person back.

Sample text

  • Women give-and-take partner
  • Slice left; men give-and-take neighbor

Grand gents chain

Description

Gents chain for more than two couples, all facing into a circle/square. Men star left halfway, and clockwise courtesy turn with someone else.

Defaults

Four couples, in a square formation. Gents chain two places, courtesy turning with their diagonal opposite.

Sample text

  • Grand gents chain to partner

Grand ladies chain

Description

Ladies chain for more than two couples, all facing into a circle/square. Women star right, and courtesy turn with someone else.

Defaults

Four couples, in a square formation. Ladies chain two places, courtesy turning with their diagonal opposite.

Sample text

  • Grand ladies chain to corner C2
  • Grand ladies chain one place to corner C2

Grand right and left

Description

In a square/circle, face someone, and pull by right. Keep going to the next person and pull by left. Repeat the pattern as many times as the dance requires.

In a contra, it is done in the major set, along the sides. (If done in groups of four, this move is called a "square through".) People that reach the top or bottom of the set must face across and wrap around the ends.

History

From square dance/quadrilles. Introduced to contra dance by Ted Sannella in "Salute to Larry Jennings."

Notation

Passes and people are listed in order. There need to be at least two pull-bys to qualify as a "grand right and left".

Sample text

  • Grand right and left (N1R;N2L;N3R)
  • Grand right and left (PR;C3L;C2R;C1L)
  • Grand right and left (P1R;P2L;P3R;P4L)

Wrong-way grand right and left

Description

A grand right and left in a square/group of eight/circle mixer where men travel clockwise, women travel counterclockwise.

Notation

Currently, no grand right-and-lefts in this database are labelled "wrong way" -- you'll have to determine this from the context of the dance.

Grapevine step

Description

A footwork pattern for sidestepping left (or right). If going to the left, step left on your left foot, then bring your right foot across and in front of the left foot, and put your weight on it. Then step left on your left foot, then bring your right foot across and behind the left foot. Repeat as long as needed. One step per beat.

The final step right is a close, rather than bringing the right foot behind/in front of the left.

Sometimes it starts with stepping on left foot, then bringing the right foot behind rather than in front.

For a grapevine step to the right, do the mirror image of all this.

Sample text

  • Facing along, shift left (grapevine step)

Half sashay

Description

Trade places with someone you're next to, finishing facing the same direction. The person on the left sidesteps to the right, while the person on the right sidesteps to the left. One goes in front of the other. It's equivalent to a half mad robin, but without the eye focus on a third party.

A full sashay is two half sashays in a row.

This can also be done as couples, typically in a four facing four.

History

Comes from early Western square dance, which had full sashays around the edges of a square.

Sample text

  • Half sashay counterclockwise around neighbor

Heel/toe

Description

A bit of footwork. Holding on to one person (either in ballroom hold, or two-hand hold), extend the foot in the direction you'll be travelling during this figure. (For example, men's left foot, women's right), and then touch the ground with your heel. Then bring the foot back, touching with your toe. This heel-and-toe can repeat, or be followed by a side-step of a step-close step.

History

From the dance "Heel and Toe Polka." Introduced into contra dance by Herbie Gaudreau, but really only used in MWSD contra.

Sample text

  • Neighbor heel; toe; step-close-step out (away from partner)

Hey

Description

A hey is a weaving figure for four people. The path is that of a numeral 8 on the floor with an extra loop on top. In a standard hey, one pair of people (for example, women) pass one shoulder in the center. Then everyone passes the other shoulder on the side. The current ends make a banking loop while the current centers pass the original shoulder in the center. Then everyone passes the other shoulder on the side. Repeat all that to place.

Starting positions can vary.

Defaults

Hey is for four people. It can start with facing couples, or in a column of four. The default orientation is across the set.

History

The oldest hey of modern contra dance that caught on was "Bonny Jean" by Ted Sannella. However, see my notes under the dance "The Rival" by Ralph Page for a possible earlier alternate. And heys in general (including a hey for four in the 1651 dance "Goddesses") predate earliest Playford.

Notation

All passes in the hey for four are listed by who passes, and by which shoulder.

Sometimes the final pass is followed by ~. That means the pass is only half-complete, until you're shoulder-to-shoulder with that person. For instance, finishing a hey in long waves, or in a circle.

  • Hey 1/4 is two passes, where half the people cross the set.
  • Hey 1/2 is three or four passes, where everyone crosses the set.
  • Hey 3/4 is five or six passes, where everyone crosses the set, and then two people return to their original side.
  • A full hey (without a number) is seven or eight passes, where everyone finishes on their original side.

Sample text

  • Hey 1/2 (WR;N2L;MR;SL~)
  • Hey 1/2 with hands (NR;ML;PR;WL)
  • On left diagonal, hey 1/2 (WR;N2L;MR;PL)
  • Hey (1R;SRNL;2R;NL;1R;SRNL;2R;NL)
  • Hey (WR;PL;MR;NL;WR;PL;MR;NL~)
  • Hey 3/4 (ML;PR;WL;NR;ML)

Lead down/up

Description

A traditional figure as done in the A2 of "Chorus Jig". The active couple goes down/up the center of the set, holding nearest hand with each other. This is normally done with the sequence of ones lead down; one turn alone/as a couple; one lead up; and ones cast off into second place.

Notation

If it's a lead down followed by a lead up to place, no distance is given. Otherwise, the couples you pass are listed in parentheses.

History

This was very popular in 19th century contras, and ECD before. In modern contra, this has been mostly replaced by the all-active in a line of four, go down the hall.

Sample text

  • Ones lead down
  • Ones lead down (past N1;N2)
  • Ones lead down to bottom

Mad robin

Description

A sideways do-si-do/seesaw. Or a full sashay with eye contact.

While facing one person, you travel in an oval around the person at your side. Typically you maintain eye contact with the person you're facing.

Notation

Who you go around is listed. A clockwise mad robin begins with the left-hand person going in front, the right-hand person going behind. A counterclockwise mad robin begins with the right-hand person going in front, the left-hand person going behind.

History

Adapted from the English Country Dance "Mad Robin". Modern interpretations of that dance added the eye contact. Contra made this an all-active figure.

Defaults

The person you face is across the set. You are travelling around the person on the side of the set.

Sample text

  • Mad robin clockwise 1 & 1/2 around neighbor N1
  • Mad robin counterclockwise around partner

Mirror/split

Description

A figure where one side of the set is doing one figure, and the other side of the set is doing the mirror image of the same figure. (For example, an allemande left on one side, and an allemande right on the other.) The mirror plane of symmetry runs down the center of the set.

Notation

The start of the figure (who splits who) tells which pair of people starts moving towards the center of the set.

Sample text

  • Neighbor mirror do-si-do (ones split twos)
  • Neighbor mirror pass through (ones split twos)
  • Neighbor mirror allemande 1 & 1/2 (ones split twos)

Mirror hey for three

Description

A six-person figure. Two heys for three, where one hey starts right-shoulder, and the other hey starts left shoulder. Couples mirror each other, getting close to each other and far away from each other.

History

From English Country Dance.

Sample text

  • Mirror hey for three along (ones split twos)

Mountain-style do-si-do

Description

A two-person figure. Two people face the same direction, hold nearest hands, and make an arch. One person stays put, with the joined hands staying above their head. The other person goes clockwise/counterclockwise around the other person. The default is once around.

History

A traditional square dance figure from eastern Kentucky/Tennessee, introduced to Berea by Pat Napier. Modern western square dance added the descriptor "Mountain style." (Thank you to David Millstone and Bob Dalsemer for this correction.)

Sample text

  • Partner Mountain-style do-si-do: Holding man's right, woman's left, women go counterclockwise around partner, men staying put, just raising arm above head

Mountaineer loop

Description

A transition from a ring to a line of four. From a circle of four, one couple arches. The other couple goes under the arch, lets go of each other, and goes around the nearest person to the end of a line of four facing the same direction as the arch. Only one handhold is broken in this figure. The archers need to turn under their own hands towards the end of this figure.

Sample text

  • Mountaineer loop: Keeping hands joined, ones arch, twos dive; twos let go of each other and separate to place

Move up

Description

An adjustment figure to keep the set of drifting down/up during a cast or some other unequal figure. When one couple casts, the other couple shifts/leads into the vacant place.

Notation

This is not always explicitly listed in the dance description. Use your best judgement.

  • Ones cast down (past twos; threes) || Others move up

Mow the wheat

Description

A composite figure of arching and diving with one other couple. It starts with two facing couples. One arches over the second, while the second ducks under. Then the second arches and the first ducks under, this time all going backwards.

There is a variant of this where the two couples are facing the same direction. In this case, for each arch/dive, one couple is going forwards while the other couple is backing up. This is probably a different figure, but I'm giving it the same search term. See Nantucket Sleigh Ride for an example.

Notation

The number following it is the number of archings in this figure.

History

Comes from traditional square dancing. The name varies but is often Mow/Sow the Wheat/Clover.

Sample text

  • Mow the wheat 3:
    (4) Twos arch, ones dive
    (4) Ones arch, twos dive (all backwards)
    (4) Twos arch, ones dive

Ricochet loop

Description

What happens when a ricochet hey has so many ricochets that it's no longer a hey, so everyone stays on their side of the set.

Dancers walk the same track as the end of a hey. Whenever they reach the center of the set, they rebound off a dancer on the other side of the set by raising two hands, pivoting against each other, and then pushing back.

The pattern is more technically the shape of a pizza wedge, with the point of the slice aimed across the set. This move is typically done always facing the same direction, similar to a mad robin.

Notation

You walk (clockwise or counterclockwise) around someone on your side of the set. You rebound off a second person, typically your same-role neighbor. The third person -- the person you can face as in a mad robin -- is not named in the figure description.

History

"Ricochet loop" is my nomenclature -- you should find your own. I added it to the glossary on 4/2021, as it got more popular in virtual contra dances.

Sample text

  • Ricochet loop clockwise against same-role neighbor (going around partner)

Ricochet hey

Description

A hey variant, where instead of two people passing by a shoulder, they raise two hands and push off each other to bounce back to the side where they started. If the hey pass in the center was going to be right shoulder, the people push back and to the left. If the hey pass in the center was going to be left shoulder, the people push back and to the right.

After the dancers ricochet, they remain facing the center of the set unless/until they cross the set. Which means after they ricochet, their next step is backwards.

Notation

Any number of passes in the center can instead be ricochets. The details of the hey indicate which couples ricochet at which point in the hey. The shoulder pass after a ricochet is from the perspective of the person moving forwards.

History

This originally started as a flourish -- if you ricochet twice in a full hey, they cancel.

Sample text

  • Ricochet hey (WR;N2L;M ricochet;N2L;W ricochet;N2L;MR)

Sashay

Description

This is a dance step -- a slipping sidestep. It is most often done as a couple, taking two hands with each other and slipping down the center of the set, as in Galopede.

This can be done slow or fast, depending on the distance covered.

The slow version of sashay is also known as "chassez". The fast version is also known as "gallop".

See also "half sashay."

Notation

The context in this database in not templated precisely. However, some guidelines:

Probably gallop/slipping tempo:

  • If "sashay" appears after a person's name, like "ones sashay down to bottom".
  • If done in a longways set like "Galopede", or some other barn/family dance.
  • If the distance to be covered is far.

Probably walking tempo:

  • If done individually, in a contra.
  • If done around someone(s) else, and has the word "around", like "Half sashay counterclockwise around neighbor".
  • If the distance to be covered is short.

Sample text

  • Ones sashay up
  • Ones sashay down to bottom
  • As couples, half sashay counterclockwise around trail buddies
  • P1 partner sashay counterclockwise

Starburst

Description

Your feet follow the track of a half poussette (or a weave the line with the sharp direction changes rounded out), but you're not touching anyone, and you're always facing forwards in the direction you're travelling. Another way to think of it is you arc around the outside of the set around one (or more) couples, coming back in to the major set in a progressed place.

Like weave the line or poussette, both you and your partner finish on the same side of the set as you started. Each person has their own individual arc -- nobody is chasing another person.

This is a progressive figure. If the starburst went all the way around back to where it started, it would be notated as two separate starbursts.

History

Described by Luke Donforth in 2019.

Notation

The number after the starburst shows the number of couples you go past as you arc outside of the set. The couples you go around are specified in parentheses.

In some cases, like the original triplet, couples can do this action without having another couple to go around.

Sample text

  • Starburst clockwise 2 (arc past N1;N2)
  • [Twos] Starburst counterclockwise 1 (arc up one place)

Description

Trade the wave

Description

A move where you loop to the other side of a wave of four. In so doing, everyone goes to the other side, centers and ends swap roles, and the handness of the wave changes.

Specifically, end people of the wave of four walk forward and curve towards the other side of the wave, finishing in the center on the other side of the wave. At the same time, center people walk forward and curve towards the other side of the set, finishing at the end on the other side of the set. These people pass right shoulders with each other to avoid colliding.

When done, you're facing the opposite direction. The hands in the center and on the ends have changed, so a right-handed wave of four turns into a left-handed wave of four. (Or a left-handed wave has turned into a right-handed wave.)

History

An MWSD (modern western square dance) figure.

Sample text

  • Trade the wave: Trade places with partner in arc (PR); form new wave, all facing other direction (NL,MR)

Description

Sample text

Turn as couples

Description

Trade place with the other specified person and face the other direction. The actual mechanics of the trading places are up to the dancers -- courtesy turn, California twirl, hand cast, or something else. This is a typical way of changing the direction of a line of four.

Notation

Hopefully I've listed who you turn with. If not, use your best judgement.

Sample text

  • Neighbor turn as couples
  • Centers turn as couples || Ends turn alone

Turn single

Description

Turn completely around in place, typically in four beats. Usually it's looping around the equivalent of a manhole cover. It finishes where it began, usually in the same orientation.

Notation

History

This is an import from English Country Dance.

Twirl to swap

Description

Trade places with the other person, raising your joined hands, the woman typically going under the arch. Finish facing another direction, which is often 180 degrees away from your original direction.

Notation

Joined hands will be specified if not clear from context of previous or following moves.

History

This is a catch-all term invented by Larry Jennings. It includes the figures California Twirl, star through, box the gnat, swat the flea, Jersey twirl, and more unusual combinations. In this database, the first five figures are explicitly named. Anything else gets labelled "Twirl to swap".

Here's how the specific twirl to swaps are broken down: (NOTE: Numerous typos in this list fixed 7/26/2022)

  • Arky star through: Men's left hand, women's right hand with the person in front of you.
  • Box the gnat: Right hand to right hand with the person in front of you.
  • California twirl: Men's right hand, women's left hand with the person at your side.
  • Jersey twirl: Men's left hand, women's right hand with the person at your side.
  • Slide through: Loosely a without-hands star through. (Technically, pass the person you face by the right, then men turn 90 degrees clockwise, women turn 90 degrees counterclockwise.)
  • Star through: Men's right hand, women's left hand with the person in front of you.
  • Swat the flea: Left hand to left hand with the person in front of you.
  • Twirl to swap: Anything else.

In modern western square dance, the starting and finishing positions are very precise. In contra, they're more fuzzy, as the two involved people will be named during the walkthrough.

Note that searching for "twirl to swap" in the database will not find things like "box the gnat". Sorry about that.

Sample text

  • Partner twirl to swap

Two-leaf clover

Description

Everyone's in the position of a star promenade, but the hand-holds are a little more unusual. I recommend watching the video below. I'll also tweak Cary Ravitz's description from the dance "Contra Clovers":

"In the initial two leaf clover all four people are in line across the set, women on the inside. One couple is together, facing up and the other couple is together, facing down."

"Men's left hands are behind their neighbor's back and right hands are across their front. Women's hands are crossed in front. Everyone is facing counter-clockwise to turn the clover."

"Then the clover shifts. Women slide back to back and men turn around in place counter-clockwise, with no raised hands. This puts partners together, facing clockwise."

History

Taken from Appalachian square dance.

Venus and Mars stars

Description

Two stars (typically four people each, traditionally one with all men, the other with all women) are next to each other, one rotating clockwise, the other rotating counterclockwise. Where the stars are closest, people swap from one star to the other, trading stars as they pass each other. Typically, all four people in turn swap stars.

Notation

Obviously this isn't a duple minor contra dance figure, but it pops up from time to time in four-face-fours, or other formations.

History

A figure from early western square dance.

Sample text

  • Venus and Mars stars: Mirror stars 1, swapping sides (women going front of partner)

Description

A star promenade that goes once around, and typically is not followed by a butterfly whirl.

As in star promenade, parentheses show the people in the center and the hands they're holding with each other.

Weave the line

Description

A zigzag-type figure, partially heying as couples along the line. This can also be thought of as a sequence of veers. Face the other couple along the set, holding nearest hand with the person at your side. With the person at your side, step forward and to the left (or right for the mirror image), and then forward and to the right (or left for the mirror image) until everyone's back in the set, all having progressed and facing the next couple. If the figure continues, it will continue forward and to the right (or left for the mirror image.)

Notation

The person you travel with is the person you do the figure with. Parentheses list when you go forward and to the left or forward and to the right, whom you pass, and how much you do.

Defaults

Finish with couples facing couples, unless indicated otherwise.

History

From the dance "Weave the Line" by Kathy Anderson.

Sample text

  • Weave the line with partner (L;R to N2)
  • Weave the line with partner (L;R to N3; R); form two-faced line
  • Weave the line with partner (R;L to N2; L;R to N3; R;L to N4; L;R to N5)

Wheel and deal

Description

A way for couples to turn around, while converting from a line of four to a column of couples.

When done in a two-faced line, couples curve forward and toward the other couple until they're facing each other, all facing the opposite of their original direction.

When done in a line of four, couples curve forward and toward the other couple until they're in a column of couples all facing the same direction, which is the opposite of their original direction. The original right-hand couple in the line of four loops a bit more so they finish in front of the other couple.

History

An MWSD figure.

Sample text

  • Wheel and deal: As couples, curve clockwise to face neighbor along the set
  • Wheel and deal: As couples, curve to face up, right-hand couple in front
  • Wheel and deal from line of four with neighbor

Wheelbarrow

Description

A poussette that doesn't go around another couple, simply staying in a slot. This is typically done in a circle mixer. Everyone stands in front of their current partner, holding two hands. One person goes forward, the other person backs up. The figure then often reverses back to place.

History

A generalized MWSD word for poussette or poussette-like figure. In this database, actual poussettes have been renamed as poussettes.

Sample text

  • P1 partner wheelbarrow (men pull women out of center)

Zipper

Description

The signature figure from Dan Pearl's "Beneficial Tradition." Pull by the person across the set from you, and the face back in. With the next person on the slight diagonal, pull by them with your other hand.

Notation

Every two pull-bys counts as once through the figure, and progresses you once. So "Zipper 2" would be a total of four pull-bys.

Details of the pull-bys are in the indented text.

History

I've made up a name for this figure just to make it easier to search for. I recommend you not call it by this name, to reduce jargon. This figure first appears in contras in "Beneficial Tradition." Dan Pearl may have adapted this figure from the 3-couple ECD set dance "Pat's Tradition."

Sample text

  • Zipper 2:
    (4) Neighbor pull by left
    (4) On right diagonal, same-role neighbor pull by right [W with N1, M with N2]
    (4) N2 neighbor pull by left
    (4) On right diagonal, same-role neighbor pull by right [W with N2, M with N3]
